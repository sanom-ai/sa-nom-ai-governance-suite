# ROI One-Pager

Use this one-pager when a prospect asks why SA-NOM is worth funding, piloting, or rolling out.

This is not a promise of guaranteed ROI.
It is a structured way to frame the business case around one workflow, one operating problem, and one deployment path.

## What SA-NOM Changes

Without SA-NOM, many organizations face some combination of:
- unmanaged AI usage across teams
- weak authority boundaries and unclear escalation paths
- manual evidence gathering during audit or review
- private-AI initiatives that stall because deployment readiness is unclear
- delays caused by legal, IT, compliance, or security uncertainty

With SA-NOM, the organization can move toward:
- governed AI roles instead of ad hoc prompting
- explicit escalation and human override paths
- tamper-evident runtime and evidence records
- private first deployment through Ollama when required
- clearer go-live readiness before production use

## ROI Usually Comes From Four Places

1. Coordination savings
- less time spent routing work manually
- fewer unclear ownership handoffs
- less management overhead during cross-functional workflows

2. Review and evidence savings
- less manual audit preparation
- faster evidence packaging
- less scramble when internal review is triggered

3. Deployment acceleration
- shorter time to prove a private or governed AI lane is ready
- fewer blocked discussions between business, IT, legal, and compliance
- faster pilot-to-rollout decisions when readiness is visible

4. Risk reduction
- lower chance of unmanaged AI use in sensitive workflows
- better control over who can publish, approve, or escalate
- fewer hidden operational gaps before go-live

## A Simple ROI Model

Build the case around one workflow first.

Use inputs like these:
- requests or cases per week
- people involved in each workflow
- average handling time today
- hours spent on review, escalation, and evidence collection
- value of reducing delay in the workflow
- cost of internal friction or audit scramble

### Back-of-Envelope Formula

A simple framing model:
- coordination savings per month = hours saved x loaded team cost
- evidence savings per review cycle = hours avoided x loaded team cost
- deployment acceleration value = estimated value of earlier safe go-live
- risk reduction value = qualitative or semi-quantitative depending on the environment

You do not need every number to be exact.
You need a credible directional case that leadership understands.

## Example ROI Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Governed internal review workflow

If one workflow currently consumes 8 to 12 staff-hours per week across operations, legal, and management coordination, reducing only part of that overhead may already create value that is easy to compare against a pilot or starter commercial package.

### Scenario 2: Audit-heavy environment

If each internal review cycle requires repeated manual evidence gathering, SA-NOM may create value by reducing repeated evidence work even before the organization rolls AI out broadly.

### Scenario 3: Private-first AI adoption

If leadership requires a private AI lane before approving AI usage in real workflows, the value is not only time saved. The value is that the initiative can move forward at all with a stronger chance of passing internal review.

## ROI Questions To Ask Leadership

- What is the cost of the current manual process each month?
- What is the cost of delay if this workflow cannot move forward safely with AI?
- Which pain is bigger right now: time, risk, evidence, or rollout friction?
- If the first deployment worked, what measurable outcome would matter most in 60 to 90 days?
- Is the decision mostly economic, compliance-driven, or strategic?

## When A Paid Pilot Makes Sense

A paid pilot is usually justified when:
- there is a real workflow owner
- there is a real deployment environment in mind
- private or regulated constraints matter
- the team needs proof in its own environment before rollout
- leadership wants decision-ready evidence, not only a generic product demo

## Pilot Outcomes That Help The ROI Story

A good pilot should prove:
- the intended provider lane is viable
- the governed workflow can run in the target environment
- stakeholders accept the role boundaries and escalation behavior
- the runtime produces evidence the organization can actually use
- the business case is strong enough to justify rollout or further scoped work

## What Not To Do

Do not pitch ROI like a generic automation fantasy.
Avoid:
- claiming hard savings without workflow context
- pitching every possible department at once
- skipping deployment or governance constraints in the business case
- treating hosted and private lanes as equivalent when privacy requirements matter
